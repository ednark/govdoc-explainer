Table 1-2 Laws and Regulations Personally Identifiable Information (PII) Personally Identifiable Information (PII) as defined in OMB Memorandum M-07-16 refers to information that can be used to distinguish or trace an individual’s identity, either alone or when combined with other personal or identifying information that is linked or linkable to a specific individual. Information that could be tied to more than one person (date of birth) is not considered PII unless it is made available with other types of information that together could render both values as PII (for example, date of birth and street address). A non-exhaustive list of examples of types of PII includes: Social Security numbers Passport numbers Driver’s license numbers Biometric information DNA information Bank account numbers Voice recordings PII refers to information that can be traced back to an individual person. Privacy Designation Cloud Service Providers (CSPs) perform an annual analysis to determine if PII is collected by any of the system components. Clouds that do not collect PII and would like to opt-out of hosting privacy information may elect to do so and are not required to fill out the Privacy Impact Assessment Questions. If a CSP is willing to host PII, the Privacy Impact Assessment Questions should be answered given the current knowledge of the CSP. A CSP is not required to solicit customers for the information. Federal cloud customers (data owner/system owners) are required to perform their own Privacy Impact Assessments, and may share this information with the CSP if they so desire (for informational purposes and/or to work with the CSP to develop processes and procedures for managing their PII).

Privacy Impact Assessment Talking Points According to NIST SP 800-122, Appendix D, There must be no personal data record-keeping systems whose very existence is secret. Additionally, NIST SP 800-122, Appendix D states, There should be a general policy of openness about developments, practices and policies with respect to personal data. Means should be readily available of establishing the existence and nature of personal data and the main purposes of their use, as well as the identity and usual residence of the data controller. In light of the NIST guidance, Privacy Impact Assessment talking points have been developed for the purpose of ensuring full disclosure between stakeholders.     Purpose Statement: Ensure our system complies with federal privacy regulations to protect people's personal information.

    • Designer: "Ensure our user interfaces respect privacy rules, making users feel their data is secure."

    • Developer: "Implement backend logic in Drupal that complies with federal privacy standards for handling personal data."

    • Project Manager: "Coordinate and guide the team to meet federal privacy standards on protecting personal information."



    From the perspective of a DevOps technician, here are the minimal actions needed to ensure compliance with the government standards, specifically focusing on privacy and Personally Identifiable Information (PII):

    1. Identify PII Collection Points:

      • Map out all application components and identify where PII is collected, stored, and processed.

    2. Implement Access Controls:

      • Set up role-based access controls (RBAC) to ensure only authorized personnel have access to PII.
      • Ensure that access to PII requires managerial approval.

    3. Data Encryption:

      • Encrypt PII both in transit and at rest using industry-standard encryption protocols.

    4. Audit and Monitoring:

      • Enable logging and monitoring to track access to PII.
      • Implement tools to alert when unauthorized access to PII occurs.

    5. Regular Security Training:

      • Ensure annual security and privacy training for all staff who have access to PII.

    6. Data Retention and Disposal:

      • Define and implement data retention policies in accordance with federal guidelines.
      • Establish secure procedures for data disposal (e.g., shredding, degaussing).

    7. Incident Response Plan:

      • Develop and maintain an incident response plan that includes actions to take if PII is compromised.

    8. Document Compliance:

      • Maintain documentation showing that these controls and processes are in place and reviewed regularly.

    9. Post Privacy Policy:

      • Ensure the privacy policy is publicly viewable and kept up to date.

    10. Contractual Agreements:

      • Verify that vendor and contractor agreements include privacy and data protection clauses as per NIST SP 800-144.

    By focusing on these actions, a DevOps technician can demonstrate compliance with the standards for managing infrastructure in support of the application while ensuring PII is protected.



    Certainly! Here are the minimal actions a Designer specializing in Human Centered Design should take to ensure compliance with the FedRAMP Privacy Impact Assessment standards:

    1. Identify and Label PII:

      • Clearly identify and label all UI elements that will collect Personally Identifiable Information (PII).

    2. Consent Mechanisms:

      • Ensure there are mechanisms (e.g., checkboxes, consent forms) for users to provide explicit consent before collecting any PII.

    3. Privacy Notice:

      • Include a clear and concise privacy notice linked prominently on all pages where PII is collected, explaining why the information is collected and how it will be used.

    4. Access Controls:

      • Implement UI elements that allow users to control their privacy settings, including the ability to view, modify, and delete their PII.

    5. Transparency:

      • Design the interface to inform users what PII is being collected, who will have access to it, and for what purposes.

    6. Data Minimization:

      • Ensure the UI only requests the minimum amount of PII necessary for the function it supports.

    7. Security Indications:

      • Use visual indicators to show that data transmission is secure, such as an SSL certificate indicator in the form of a lock icon.

    8. Error Handling:

      • Provide clear error messages when PII input does not meet specified criteria to ensure accurate data collection.

    9. User Access Requests:

      • Include interfaces for users to submit requests to view, correct, or delete their PII.

    10. Retention Information:

      • Clearly display information about PII retention policies and how long the data will be stored.

    Each action ensures that the user's privacy is respected and that the application complies with FedRAMP PIA requirements.



    To show compliance with FedRAMP standards from the perspective of a Drupal developer working on backend business logic, the minimal list of actions is as follows:

    1. Identify PII:

      • Determine what types of Personally Identifiable Information (PII) will be collected, stored, or processed by the application.

    2. Access Control:

      • Implement role-based access controls (RBAC) to ensure only authorized personnel can access PII.
      • Ensure access to PII requires approval from a manager.

    3. PII Data Mapping:

      • Map all data fields that will store PII and ensure they are properly marked and protected.

    4. Encryption:

      • Implement encryption for PII both at rest and in transit.

    5. Data Minimization:

      • Collect only the minimum necessary PII required for the application's functionality.

    6. Audit Logs:

      • Enable logging to track access and modifications to PII data.

    7. Privacy Safeguards:

      • Implement measures to prevent the unauthorized browsing or misuse of PII by users with access.

    8. Contracts and Agreements:

      • Ensure that all code libraries and third-party services are compliant with FedRAMP standards.

    9. Data Accuracy and Retention:

      • Build mechanisms for data validation to ensure the accuracy of PII.
      • Implement data retention policies and ensure proper disposal of PII after the retention period.

    10. Transparency:

      • Make sure the application's privacy policy is accessible and the use of PII is transparent to users.

    11. Training:

      • As part of the development cycle, ensure that any team member with access to PII receives proper privacy and security training.

    By addressing these areas, a developer can contribute effectively to ensuring FedRAMP compliance for a Drupal-based web application.



    To ensure compliance with the FedRAMP Privacy Impact Assessment (PIA) standards, a project manager should focus on the following key actions:

    1. Identify Privacy Officer (POC):

      • Assign a Privacy Officer who will act as the Point of Contact.

    2. Understand Applicable Laws and Regulations:

      • Review relevant laws and regulations as outlined on www.fedramp.gov.

    3. Determine PII Involvement:

      • Establish if the application will collect, store, or process Personally Identifiable Information (PII).

    4. PII Mapping:

      • Create documentation mapping PII to specific application components.

    5. Prospective PII Use:

      • Clarify the sources and purpose of any PII being collected or processed.

    6. Access and Sharing:

      • Define who will have access to PII, including third-party organizations and internal personnel, and document the criteria and procedures for access.

    7. Safeguards and Liabilities:

      • Implement and document controls to prevent misuse of PII, and assign responsibility for PII protection.

    8. Contracts and Agreements:

      • Ensure contracts with customers outline PII ownership, privacy responsibilities, notification requirements, and liability terms.

    9. Accuracy and Redress:

      • Establish processes for verifying and maintaining the accuracy of PII and for allowing individuals to correct or amend their information.

    10. Retention and Disposal:

      • Define retention periods for PII and procedures for secure disposal after the retention period.

    11. Monitor and Track Access:

      • Set up mechanisms to monitor and track access to PII.

    12. Privacy Policy:

      • Develop a privacy policy, ensure it is communicated to all relevant individuals, and make it publicly viewable.

    13. Review and Sign Off:

      • Conduct internal reviews and obtain sign-offs from the Privacy Officer and Chief Privacy Officer.

    By focusing on these actions, a project manager can effectively track and organize the necessary work to guide the client towards meeting FedRAMP PIA standards.
